# Golden Lap — Product & Implementation Brief  ## Objective  Improve `golden-lap.html`, a single-file vanilla HTML/CSS/JavaScript racing team-management game, so that qualifying, race simulation, pit lanes, car development, traffic behavior, timing UI, and economy work together as a coherent motorsport management experience.  The goal is not to rewrite the game from scratch. The goal is to evolve the existing codebase into a more believable, strategic, and polished racing-manager simulation while preserving the current retro motorsport identity, top-down racing style, and season-management loop.  The target experience is a compact but convincing motorsport management game: the player should feel like a team principal managing two cars through qualifying, race strategy, car development, reliability, pit stops, and season progression.  ---  # Core Design Philosophy  Race and qualifying should feel like two expressions of the same sport, not two unrelated systems.  The game should present a believable live motorsport world where cars exist on track, drivers make decisions, traffic matters, pit lanes matter, tyres and parts matter, and time controls let the player manage long sessions without breaking immersion.  The simulation does not need to be a hardcore racing simulator. It should remain readable, stylized, and playable. But it should avoid behavior that feels fake, such as cars teleporting in place of pit-lane flow, qualifying times appearing disconnected from cars on track, drivers repeatedly colliding because they lack spatial awareness, or two team cars sharing one invisible global car state.  The player should understand what is happening and why. When a driver loses time, finds traffic, pits, sets a lap, suffers wear, or benefits from a better part, the game should feel internally consistent.

 # Implementation Priorities  Work in this order of importance:  1. Build a coherent live session flow shared by race and qualifying. 2. Make every track support a believable pit-lane experience. 3. Set race distance from an approximately 60-minute target at 1x speed. 4. Improve driver racecraft, traffic behavior, overtaking, defending, and collision avoidance. 5. Replace the shared car development model with two independent cars and interchangeable parts. 6. Refine timing panels, workshop UI, mobile readability, balance, and session edge cases.  Avoid solving a lower-priority feature in a way that makes a higher-priority feature brittle. Prefer integrated design over isolated patches.  ---  # 1. Shared Live Session Model  ## Intent  Qualifying and race should use the same live session foundation wherever practical.  Both sessions should feel like they happen on the same track, with the same cars, the same physics, the same pit-lane world, and the same sense of time passing. The player should not feel that qualifying is a spreadsheet screen and race is the “real” game.  ## Desired Feel  A race weekend should flow naturally:  * The team prepares for qualifying. * Cars leave the garage or pit lane. * Drivers set qualifying laps in traffic and clean air. * Qualifying produces a grid. * The race starts from that grid. * Race strategy unfolds over a meaningful distance. * Results affect money, points, part wear, and workshop decisions. * The next round continues the season.  The player should always feel oriented. Session transitions should be clean and deliberate, with no stale HUD, hidden state, or leftover UI from the previous phase.  ---  # 2. Live Qualifying  ## Intent  Qualifying should become a proper 30-minute live session, not a short accelerated management screen.  A qualifying attempt should feel like a motorsport run: the car leaves the garage or pit lane, builds up to speed, finds space, starts a flying lap, completes a timed lap, then either continues, cools down, boxes, or prepares for another attempt.

Desired Feel  Qualifying should create strategic tension without becoming overly complex.  The player should care about:  * When to send each car out. * Whether there is enough time for another run. * Whether the car has clean air. * Whether traffic will compromise the lap. * Whether tuning or setup decisions are worth pursuing. * Whether tyres and track conditions favor going now or waiting.  The session should last 30 in-game minutes and default to 1x speed. The player should be able to pause, slow time, and speed time up. Time controls should feel consistent with the race.  Qualifying laps should primarily come from cars actually running on the live track. Rival cars may use reasonable abstraction or fallback only when needed to keep the session from producing broken or empty results. Any abstraction should support the live-session illusion rather than replace it.  ## Session Outcome  Qualifying results should determine the race grid. Cars without valid representative laps should be handled gracefully and believably, not in a way that breaks the weekend flow.  ---  # 3. Race Duration and Lap Count  ## Intent  Races should feel like real team-management events rather than short sprints.  At 1x speed, each race should target approximately 60 minutes of in-game race time.  ## Desired Feel  Race length should vary naturally by track. A short circuit should produce more laps, and a longer circuit should produce fewer laps. The lap count should be derived from an expected leader race pace, not from a hard-coded one-size-fits-all value.  The expected lap time should represent realistic race pace, not a perfect theoretical qualifying lap. It may be based on the existing track speed profile, but it should account for the difference between an ideal lap and a believable race lap.  Time acceleration is essential. A 60-minute 1x race should be possible for simulation credibility, but 4x and 8x should remain stable and usable for normal play and testing.  ---  # 4. Pit Lanes on Every Track  ## Intent  Every track should have a believable and functional pit lane.  The pit lane should be part of the racing world, not a visual decoration or a teleport destination.  ## Desired Feel  A car entering the pits should visibly leave the racing line, slow into the pit lane, reach its service area, complete its stop or garage action, then rejoin through a coherent pit exit.  Pit behavior should feel appropriate in both race and qualifying:  In race sessions, the pit lane should support tyre changes, repairs, strategy calls, time loss, and rejoining traffic.  In qualifying sessions, the pit lane should support garage release, out laps, hot laps, in laps, boxing the car, and managing traffic around pit exit.  The existing pit apron, pit boxes, or pit point can be reused or evolved, but the final result should feel like a real pit-lane flow. The player should not see cars vanish, snap into place, or behave as though the pit lane is not part of the track.  Each track should retain its own identity. Pit lanes should fit naturally into the circuit layout and visual style.  ---
5. Driver Racecraft and Traffic Behavior  ## Intent  Drivers should feel like racing drivers, not slot cars following a single line until they collide.  They should understand nearby cars, choose sensible lines, make passes when opportunities exist, defend in believable ways, and avoid unnecessary contact.  ## Desired Race Feel  In races, faster cars should be able to overtake slower cars without requiring constant contact. Passing should not be trivial, but it should happen naturally when pace difference, track position, and racing line make it plausible.  Drivers should show basic racecraft:  * Closing on slower cars. * Choosing a side to attack. * Holding a line when alongside. * Leaving enough room to avoid unrealistic crashes. * Defending without weaving unnaturally. * Backing out when the move is not realistic. * Recovering from compromised lines. * Occasionally making mistakes or light contact where it adds drama.  The goal is not perfectly sterile racing. The goal is believable racing. Contact should feel like an incident, not the normal way cars interact.  ## Desired Qualifying Feel  In qualifying, drivers should behave differently from race mode.  A driver on a hot lap should seek clean air and avoid unnecessary battles. A driver on an out lap, in lap, or compromised lap should avoid ruining another driver’s timed lap where practical.  Traffic should create strategic pressure: the player may mistime a release or catch a slower car. But it should not feel like random chaos or unavoidable crashing.  ---  # 6. Collision Avoidance and Natural Recovery  ## Intent  The best fix for stuck or entangled cars is smarter driving and more believable physical interaction, not a visibly artificial rescue system.  Cars should usually avoid getting into bad contact states in the first place. When contact does happen, they should separate and continue in a way that feels natural.  ## Desired Feel  A close fight should look like two drivers negotiating space. A mistake should look like a racing incident. A recovery should look like drivers correcting their cars and continuing, not like objects being forcibly pulled apart by hidden code.  The simulation should avoid situations where cars repeatedly push each other, lock together, oscillate side to side, or remain trapped at the edge of the track or pit lane.  If recovery logic is needed, it should be subtle and serve the illusion of better driver behavior. It should not create obvious teleporting, chaotic chain reactions, or unrealistic snapping except as a last-resort failsafe.
 # 7. Driver Position and Timing Panel  ## Intent  The driver position panel should provide enough timing context for the player to manage the race without overwhelming the screen.  ## Desired Feel  The panel should show who is where, what their current race status is, and how their pace is developing.  It should include:  * Current position. * Driver identifier or name. * Gap or status. * Latest lap time. * Fastest lap time.  Drivers without a completed lap should show a clear placeholder.  The layout should remain compact and readable. Desktop can show more information; mobile should prioritize legibility and avoid cramming too much into a narrow tower.  The panel should work naturally with both live qualifying and race sessions.  ---  # 8. Two Independent Cars and Interchangeable Parts  ## Intent  The player should manage two real cars, not one shared abstract car.  Each driver should have a car with its own installed components, condition, strengths, weaknesses, and development path.  ## Desired Feel  The team should feel like it owns and maintains a small inventory of real racing parts.  Each car should have:  * Engine. * Aero. * Chassis.  Each part should have its own identity, development level, and condition. If a part is worn, upgraded, repaired, or swapped, that should follow the part itself. A strong engine moved from Car 1 to Car 2 should remain the same strong engine. A worn chassis should remain worn until repaired.  The player should be able to make meaningful team-management choices:  * Prioritize one driver with the better parts. * Balance both cars evenly. * Risk a worn part to save money. * Repair reliability before a race. * Upgrade for pace. * Swap parts after qualifying or before the next event where appropriate. * Manage the long-term cost of running two cars.  ## Performance Meaning  Part types should remain intuitive:  * Engine affects power or acceleration. * Aero affects cornering, grip, or downforce-related performance. * Chassis affects drag, stability, top speed, or structural reliability.  The model can stay simple, but it should be coherent. The player should be able to understand why a car is faster, slower, more fragile, or more expensive to maintain.  ---  # 9. Wear, Repairs, Reliability, and Damage  ## Intent  Reliability and repair decisions should feel like part of running a race team.  The player should understand the difference between long-term part condition, race damage, pit-lane fixes, and workshop repairs.  ## Desired Feel  Parts should wear through use. A car that races hard, pushes more, suffers incidents, or runs long sessions should feel like it puts more strain on its components.  Workshop repairs should feel like proper restoration of part condition between sessions or rounds.  Pit-lane repairs during a race should feel like emergency service: useful, costly in time, and possibly limited compared with full workshop repair. They should not create confusing contradictions where long-term wear disappears without explanation.  Reliability failures should create tension, but not feel arbitrary or unfair. The player should be able to read risk through part condition, strategy, and prior decisions.
 # 10. Tuning and Qualifying Relationship  ## Intent  Tuning should feel connected to qualifying and race preparation, not like a disconnected minigame.  ## Desired Feel  Qualifying should help the team learn, refine, or exploit setup in a way that supports the wider race weekend. Tuning should matter, but it should not undermine the new independent-car model.  The chosen design may attach tuning to the team, to each car, to installed parts, to race setup, or to another coherent abstraction. The important requirement is consistency.  Tuning should not duplicate, disappear, or attach to the wrong object when parts are swapped. The player should understand what the tuning work improved and why it affects performance.  The system can remain lightweight and stylized, but it should feel like part of preparing racing cars.  ---  # 11. Weather, Tyres, and Track Conditions  ## Intent  Weather and tyres should create strategic texture without misleading the player.  ## Desired Feel  If the game presents a weather forecast, that forecast should matter or be clearly framed. If rain and wetness affect only the race, the UI should not imply they are central to qualifying. If they affect both sessions, the behavior should feel coherent.  Tyres should remain meaningful:  * Softs should feel fast but fragile. * Hards should feel durable but slower. * Wets should matter when the track is wet.  The longer race duration means tyre wear, fuel, pit windows, rain timing, and reliability need to be considered together. Strategy should become richer, not broken.
 # 12. Economy and Balance  ## Intent  The economy should support meaningful racing-team decisions.  The move to two independent cars and longer races will naturally increase maintenance pressure. That pressure is desirable only if it creates interesting tradeoffs, not if it simply makes the game punitive.  ## Desired Feel  The player should frequently face good dilemmas:  * Repair now or risk failure. * Upgrade for performance or save for later. * Give the best parts to the lead driver or spread resources evenly. * Run aggressive pace and accept higher wear. * Choose a sponsor that matches realistic team goals. * Decide whether a damaged or worn part is still worth using.  Money should matter, but the player should not feel trapped by unavoidable costs caused only by the expanded part system.  Review sponsor income, prize money, repair costs, upgrade costs, part wear, reliability failures, and race length together so the season remains playable.  ---  # 13. Session Flow, Robustness, and Edge Cases  ## Intent  The game should feel stable and coherent through a full season.  Edge cases should be handled in service of the player experience. The player should not be able to break the weekend flow through normal play.  ## Desired Feel  A full round should work smoothly from qualifying through race results and workshop decisions. Cars may fail, crash, miss laps, or run out of time, but the game should always produce a believable next step.  The game should gracefully handle unusual situations such as:  * A qualifying session ending while cars are still on track. * A driver failing to set a representative time. * A race with many retirements. * A player car failing before the finish. * Parts being swapped between sessions. * Pit-lane traffic creating awkward releases. * Mobile layouts with limited space. * Long sessions at accelerated speed.  The player should see coherent results, not freezes, broken classifications, missing UI, or console errors.
 # Constraints  The implementation must:  * Remain a single-file vanilla HTML/CSS/JavaScript game. * Use no external dependencies. * Use no build step. * Preserve the current retro motorsport visual style. * Preserve the core season/team-management loop. * Preserve session-only behavior and reset-on-reload behavior. * Avoid a full rewrite unless a limited refactor is clearly justified. * Prefer maintainable, readable code over hacks. * Avoid removing major existing features. * Keep the game playable on desktop and mobile.  Do not add persistence unless necessary. The current game communicates that progress resets on page reload, and that behavior should remain intact.  ---  # Acceptance Goals  ## Qualifying  Qualifying should feel like a real live session. It should last 30 in-game minutes, default to 1x speed, show the track, show cars running, support time controls, allow player cars to run believable qualifying attempts, and produce a race grid from timed laps.  ## Race  Race mode should still work after qualifying and should feel like the same simulation under race rules. Race distance should target approximately 60 minutes of expected leader race time at 1x speed, with lap count varying naturally by track.  ## Pit Lanes  Every track should have a visible and functional pit-lane experience. Cars should enter, service or box, and exit in a way that feels integrated with the circuit.  ## Driver Behavior  Drivers should demonstrate believable racecraft. They should overtake, defend, yield in qualifying where appropriate, avoid unnecessary collisions, and recover from incidents in a natural-looking way.  ## Timing UI  The timing panel should show position, driver identity, gap or status, latest lap, and fastest lap while remaining readable on desktop and mobile.  ## Cars and Parts  The team should clearly have two independent cars with engine, aero, and chassis parts. Parts should be swappable, individually upgradeable, individually repairable, and individually worn through use.  ## Economy  The economy should remain playable and strategically interesting after longer races and two-car maintenance are introduced.  ## Stability  A full season flow should be playable without console errors, frozen sessions, broken classifications, stale UI, or impossible progression states.  ---  # Expected Delivery  Output the updated golden-lap.html
